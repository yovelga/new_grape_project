\begin{abstract}

\noindent
Grape cracking is a severe physiological disorder that leads to substantial economic losses in table-grape production by reducing marketable yield and increasing susceptibility to secondary infections such as sour rot. Early, non-destructive detection is therefore critical for enabling timely management interventions. This study investigates the feasibility of hyperspectral imaging (HSI; VNIR 400--1000~nm) combined with machine learning for crack detection under field conditions, considering both early- and late-stage acquisitions (initial/minimal vs.\ advanced cracking). All spectral analyses were restricted to 450--925~nm and employed per-spectrum Standard Normal Variate (SNV) normalization.

A multi-stage analytical framework was developed, including pixel-level classification, whole-image crack detection, and wavelength selection. Pixel-level spectral signatures of healthy and cracked berries were manually annotated and used to train an XGBoost classifier. This model achieved high discrimination on isolated, pure pixels (accuracy $\approx$ 97\%, AUC $\approx$ 0.99); while these idealized metrics do not reflect cluttered field conditions, they successfully confirmed the fundamental physical existence of a distinct spectral separation between tissue states.

\medskip
\noindent
Direct application of pixel-level predictions to field scenes resulted in background-induced misclassification. To address this limitation, a whole-image crack-detection pipeline was developed, aggregating pixel-level CRACK probabilities using spatial post-processing (thresholding, morphological filtering, and patch/blob-based decision rules). The system was evaluated using a spatially disjoint row-based calibration--test split and reported with emphasis on the Cracked class. On the held-out test row, the pipeline achieved F1\textsubscript{CRACK} = \FoneEarlyBaseline{} under early-stage acquisition and F1\textsubscript{CRACK} = \FoneLateBaseline{} under late-stage acquisition (Table~\ref{tab:full_image_early_late_metrics}).

\medskip
\noindent
Wavelength selection experiments demonstrated that high detection performance can be maintained using a small, specific subset of wavelengths. This indicates the clear potential to replace expensive hyperspectral cameras with simpler, low-cost multispectral sensors for everyday field deployment. Overall, the results establish the feasibility of HSI-based crack detection under realistic vineyard conditions and provide a methodological foundation for operational, non-destructive monitoring tools.

\medskip
\noindent
Specifically, rigorous feature selection reduced the required data to just 30 key wavelengths, reinforcing the commercial viability of this approach. Furthermore, when tested on images from entirely new vineyard rows, the pipeline successfully identified cracks while ignoring complex background clutter and lighting changes, confirming its robustness to real-world field conditions.
\end{abstract}
