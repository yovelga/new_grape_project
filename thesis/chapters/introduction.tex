\chapter{Introduction}

\section{The Problem of Fruit Cracking}
Fruit cracking is a significant physiological disorder affecting a wide range of commercially important crops, including sweet cherries, tomatoes, pomegranates, and particularly table grapes (Vitis vinifera L.). The phenomenon is characterized by the rupture of the fruit's skin (cuticle and epidermis), exposing the internal flesh to the environment. These cracks render the fruit unmarketable and create entry points for pathogens, leading to rapid decay from secondary infections such as sour rot. Cracking typically occurs during the final stages of fruit ripening, a period when the fruit is most valuable. It is primarily caused by an imbalance between the turgor pressure within the fruit and the mechanical strength of its skin, a condition often exacerbated by environmental factors like rain, high humidity, and irrigation practices.

\section{Economic Impact}
\noindent
The economic consequences of grape cracking are substantial. Direct losses can amount to up to 30\% of the marketable yield. For table grapes, this translates to an estimated financial loss of €625 per ton, culminating in a total industry loss of approximately €175 million annually, based on European market data (Table \ref{tab:economic_impact}). Beyond direct yield loss, cracking negatively impacts fruit appearance and quality, leading to price reductions of 20-50\% and a shortened shelf-life, further diminishing profitability for growers.

\section{Research Motivation}
\medskip
\noindent
Current methods for managing cracking risk are reactive and often insufficient. There is a critical need for a non-destructive, reliable method to detect early-stage crack-related spectral signatures at the time of measurement, when cracking is minimal and visually indistinct at the field scale. Hyperspectral imaging (HSI) has emerged as a powerful tool in precision agriculture for assessing plant health and fruit quality due to its ability to capture detailed spectral information beyond the spectrum visible to the human eye. This research aims to investigate the potential of HSI and machine learning to address the challenge of grape cracking.

\medskip
\noindent
This thesis addresses two related settings. \textit{Identification/detection} focuses on existing crack tissue and its hyperspectral signature. \textit{Early-warning screening}, in contrast, addresses early-stage field assessment under realistic vineyard conditions.

\medskip
\noindent
In such scenes, direct pixel-wise segmentation is often unstable. Background clutter (e.g., leaves, wood, and artificial materials), together with changes in illumination and viewing geometry, can strongly affect pixel-level outputs and make image-level decisions unreliable. For this reason, the early-warning setting relies on image-level or cluster-level representations that aggregate information across the acquisition rather than on individual pixels alone.

\medskip
\noindent
Unlike late-stage cracking, which shows clear structural damage, early-stage defects have weak visual cues. Early-warning detection therefore uses HSI to capture subtle spectral changes before macroscopic symptoms become clearly visible.

\medskip
\noindent
To ensure clarity throughout this analysis, the following core terms are defined based on the status of the tissue at the time of measurement:

\begin{description}[style=multiline, leftmargin=3cm, font=\bfseries]
    \item[Early-stage] The phase representing the initial observable onset of cracking, captured in the earliest available image of a compromised cluster.
    \item[Late-stage] An advanced cracking condition where the damage is already well-established and more severe.
\end{description}

\medskip
\noindent
It is important to emphasize that both terms describe the \textbf{physical status at the time of acquisition}. This thesis focuses on identifying existing crack-related spectral signatures rather than predicting future outcomes or pre-symptomatic conditions.

\subsection{Problem Statement and Research Gap}

\noindent
While HSI shows strong potential for agricultural anomaly detection, most existing work has been conducted under controlled laboratory conditions or on detached fruit. A critical gap remains in translating these methods to real vineyard conditions, where background clutter, changing illumination, and other sources of variability make direct pixel-level approaches unreliable for detecting early-stage cracking in intact clusters.

\medskip
\noindent
To address this gap, this thesis proposes a spatial-aggregation framework combined with dimensionality reduction, shifting the analysis from controlled laboratory settings toward a practical, field-ready monitoring strategy.

\subsection{Research Objectives and Sub-Questions}

The goal of this research is to move from theoretical spectral analysis to a practical tool for vineyard crack detection. This objective is broken down into three logical layers:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Pixel-Level Discrimination:} To establish the fundamental ability to distinguish between healthy and cracked tissue based on spectral signatures.
    \begin{itemize}[label=--, leftmargin=*]
        \item \textit{RQ1:} Can machine learning models accurately identify cracking at its earliest observable onset, and how does this performance compare to the detection of advanced damage?
    \end{itemize}

    \item \textbf{Full-Scene Robustness:} To develop a detection pipeline that operates on whole-image acquisitions while overcoming environmental "noise" (field clutter).
    \begin{itemize}[label=--, leftmargin=*]
        \item \textit{RQ2:} How can spatial processing techniques filter out background interference (leaves, wood, lighting) to prevent false positives and enable reliable screening in the vineyard?
    \end{itemize}

    \item \textbf{Spectral Redundancy and Sensor Optimization:} To evaluate the feasibility of transitioning from high-dimensional hyperspectral data to a simplified multispectral configuration.
    \begin{itemize}[label=--, leftmargin=*]
        \item \textit{RQ3:} To what extent can detection accuracy be maintained using a reduced subset of stable wavelengths, and can this reduction support the development of affordable, few-channel sensing apparatuses?
    \end{itemize}
\end{enumerate}

    \item \textbf{In-Field Dataset \& SAM2 Annotation:} Collected a comprehensive hyperspectral dataset directly in the vineyard. To handle the complexity of labeling realistic agricultural scenes, SAM2 (Segment Anything Model 2) was utilized to streamline and generate precise masks for full-scene images, alongside manually labeled isolated pixels.
    
    \item \textbf{Analysis of Classification Limits:} Evaluated the inherent limitations of direct pixel-level classification in realistic vineyard scenes. Identified key error sources that persist despite standard spectral preprocessing—such as mixed pixels at object boundaries and spectrally confounding backgrounds—motivating a critical shift toward spatial aggregation (Chapter 6, Section 6.2).
    
    \item \textbf{Whole-Image Detection Pipeline:} Developed a comprehensive, robust crack-detection pipeline that moves beyond isolated pixels by integrating pixel-level inference, probability thresholding, and morphological filtering. The pipeline was successfully evaluated under both early-stage (minimal) and late-stage (advanced) cracking conditions (Chapter 5, Section 5.6).
    
    \item \textbf{Wavelength Selection for Practical Sensors:} Identified stable, compact wavelength subsets that significantly reduce spectral dimensionality while preserving strong detection performance. This provides a direct, practical foundation for designing simpler, cost-effective multispectral sensors for field use (Chapter 5, Sections 5.7 and 5.7.3).
\end{itemize}
