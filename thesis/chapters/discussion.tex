\section{Physiological and Pre-processing Foundations}
\label{sec:physiology_preprocessing}

\subsection{The Biological Basis of Spectral Signatures}
\noindent
The finding that cracked grape tissues exhibit lower reflectance across the 450--925\,nm range is consistent with the known optical consequences of fruit cracking.

\medskip\noindent
In the visible range, reduced reflectance is compatible with pigment-related and biochemical changes following cuticle rupture, including exposure and enzymatic browning of mesocarp tissue \parencite{Wang2015FruitQualitySpectroscopy, Gitelson1994ChlorophyllReflectance, santos2023molecular, Moon2020EnzymaticBrowning}. Consistent with this interpretation, stable wavelength selection identified multiple bins in the blue--green and visible-red portions of the spectrum, including 450\,nm, 515--580\,nm, 605\,nm, and 635\,nm.

\medskip\noindent
A particularly strong concentration of selected bins appeared in the red-edge and lower-NIR transition region, including 730, 735, 740, 755, 760, 770, and 780\,nm. This region is sensitive to both pigment concentration and tissue integrity, and its repeated selection is consistent with structural and biochemical disruption in cracked fruit tissue \parencite{Lu2020OpticalPropertiesReview, Imran2020RedEdgeNIRShoulder}.

\medskip\noindent
In the upper NIR, additional stable bins were identified at 840, 900, 915, and 920\,nm. These wavelengths are consistent with structural scattering effects and with the rising shoulder of the $\sim$970\,nm water-absorption feature \parencite{Cheng2019AppleDRS, Sun2021PeachBruisingOptical, Munera2021PersimmonDamageHSI}. Together, these selected regions support the interpretation that the observed separability reflects a combination of pigment oxidation, tissue disruption, and localized water loss.

\subsection{Data Normalization and Pre-processing Restraint}

\medskip
\noindent
Although the physiological differences between cracked and healthy tissue provided a theoretical basis for detection, recovering these signatures under field conditions required a restrained preprocessing strategy. The final pipeline was designed to reduce sensor- and acquisition-related variability while preserving the physical structure of the measured signal.

\subsubsection{Per-Spectrum Normalization}

\noindent
Field acquisition introduced strong illumination variability due to canopy shading and changing viewing geometry. To reduce this effect, Standard Normal Variate (SNV) normalization was applied on a per-spectrum basis. By normalizing each spectrum relative to its own mean and variance, SNV emphasized spectral shape over absolute brightness and thereby reduced sensitivity to illumination-induced variation (Figure~\ref{fig:snv_effect_disc}).

This restrained preprocessing strategy likely contributed to the stability of the detected spectral signatures under field conditions by limiting unnecessary alteration of the measured signal.

\section{Managing Field Clutter: The Role of Spatial Aggregation}
\label{sec:spatial_aggregation}

\subsection{Overcoming Spectral Ambiguity: The Dry Wood Challenge}
\noindent
The transition from pixel-level classification to whole-image field scenes exposed the limitations of a simple binary formulation. When the full optical heterogeneity of the vineyard background was grouped into a single \textit{non-crack} class, the resulting decision boundary became unstable and produced widespread false positives. Reformulating the task as a 10-class problem substantially reduced this noise by explicitly modeling common background elements such as leaves, soil, wood, and plastic markers, although localized false positives still remained (Figure~\ref{fig:multi_vs_binary}).

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering

    \caption[Multi-class vs.\ binary crack-probability maps]{Multi-class vs.\ binary crack-probability maps for a healthy cluster. Left: RGB reference; Centre: multi-class overlay; Right: binary overlay. The binary model produces widespread false positives, whereas the multi-class formulation confines detections to localised regions.}
    \label{fig:multi_vs_binary}
\end{figure}
\FloatBarrier

\noindent
Visual inspection of the whole-image outputs showed that the remaining false positives were driven mainly by dry woody branches, and occasionally by plastic markers. This confusion is biologically plausible: cracked tissue and dry wood both exhibit reduced water content and oxidized tissue, yielding similar VIS--NIR signatures under field conditions.

\subsection{Geometric Filtering and Structural Regularization}
\noindent
To address this residual spectral ambiguity, the pipeline incorporated spatial and geometric context. True berry cracks appeared as dense, compact pixel clusters on the berry surface, whereas branch-related responses appeared as elongated or diffuse structures. The final whole-image pipeline therefore applied morphological filtering to consolidate dense crack responses into blobs, followed by patch-based density evaluation to retain only spatially concentrated regions (Figure~\ref{fig:whole_image_pipeline_2}).

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{figures/materials_methods/pipeline/whole_image_pipeline.jpg}
    \caption[Whole-image spatial aggregation pipeline overview]{Overview of the whole-image spatial aggregation pipeline, from raw pixel-level probability maps through morphological filtering and patch-based density evaluation to the final image-level detection decision.}
    \label{fig:whole_image_pipeline_2}
\end{figure}
\FloatBarrier

\noindent
This post-processing logic was intentionally kept simple. More elaborate geometric descriptors could improve calibration performance, but they also increase the risk of overfitting to row-specific artifact shapes. By relying on blob formation and patch density alone, the pipeline acted as a structural regularizer that preserved generalization to unseen vines.

\medskip\noindent
In practice, this spatial aggregation stage was essential: it removed most low-density false-positive responses associated with thin branches, while retaining concentrated crack-like regions. Thick dry branches remained the main source of whole-image errors, but the results showed that robust field-level detection required the combination of pixel-level spectral predictions with simple spatial constraints.

\subsection{Feature Selection and Multispectral Sensor Design}
\noindent
A central objective of this study was to determine whether hyperspectral crack detection could be reduced to a practical subset of wavelengths suitable for field deployment. The BFS analysis showed that the original 159-band representation contained substantial redundancy, and that strong detection performance could be maintained using a much smaller subset of bands.

\medskip\noindent
Across repeated runs, the 30-wavelength configuration provided the most favorable balance between stability and detection performance. The full 159-band representation introduced unnecessary complexity, whereas the extreme 11-wavelength subset was less stable across fields. By contrast, the 30-wavelength subset preserved strong early-stage detection performance while reducing the feature space to a scale more compatible with practical deployment.

\medskip\noindent
This finding has direct implications for sensor design. Rather than relying on a fragile and expensive full-range hyperspectral system, the selected wavelength regions support the feasibility of a dedicated multispectral camera based on a limited number of fixed optical filters. In this sense, the wavelength-selection results provide a practical bridge between hyperspectral proof-of-concept analysis and deployable precision-viticulture hardware.

\medskip\noindent
The stability analysis also clarified the nature of spectral collinearity. Exact wavelength matching across random seeds showed some variance, but bin-level agreement increased markedly when adjacent bands were grouped into narrow spectral windows. This indicates that much of the apparent variation reflected substitution among highly correlated neighboring bands rather than instability in the underlying physiological signal.


% \subsection{Methodological Rationale: BFS and Evaluation Metrics}

% The methodological choice of Backward Feature Selection (BFS) over alternative dimensionality reduction strategies was fundamentally driven by the physical constraints of sensor design. % Similarly, while heuristic search methods like Genetic Algorithms (GA) can optimize high-dimensional spaces, their stochastic nature (relying on random mutation and crossover) produces non-deterministic subsets that fluctuate wildly between runs, hindering physiological interpretation. This traceability enabled the identification of the performance ``sweet spot'' across the elimination continuum, ensuring that the selected subsets are not random mathematical artifacts, but stable, physically meaningful markers.

% The explicit choice to optimize the BFS algorithm based on the Area Under the Precision-Recall Curve (PR--AUC) rather than the standard Area Under the Receiver Operating Characteristic Curve (ROC--AUC) was dictated by the extreme class imbalance inherent to the problem. \subsection{Methodological Rationale: BFS and Evaluation Metrics}
\noindent
Backward Feature Selection (BFS) was chosen because the objective was not only dimensionality reduction, but identification of physically realizable wavelengths for sensor design. Methods such as PCA were not suitable for this purpose, because they generate synthetic components rather than directly usable spectral bands. BFS, by contrast, preserves the original wavelengths and therefore allows direct translation to fixed-filter hardware.

\medskip\noindent
The wavelength-selection procedure was optimized using PR--AUC rather than ROC--AUC, because crack detection is intrinsically highly imbalanced. In field scenes, cracked pixels represent a small minority, and PR--AUC therefore provides a more relevant criterion for selecting wavelengths that truly support detection of the positive class rather than simply rewarding correct classification of the dominant background.

\subsection{The Limits of Unsupervised Anomaly Detection}
\noindent
The comparison between supervised XGBoost and unsupervised Autoencoder models clarified an important property of the dataset: the non-crack class was too heterogeneous to define a useful notion of ``normality.'' When the autoencoder was trained on the full non-crack background, the learned latent space became overly broad and permissive, allowing true crack spectra to be reconstructed with low error.

\medskip\noindent
Training the autoencoder on the crack class instead yielded stronger pixel-level separation, because the defect manifold was much narrower. However, this strategy proved brittle when applied to unseen field scenes, suggesting limited generalization beyond the specific crack signatures present in the calibration set. In practice, the supervised multi-class framework was more effective because it learned explicit boundaries between cracks and diverse background materials rather than relying on reconstruction failure as an indirect anomaly signal.

\medskip\noindent
This comparison also highlights the trade-off introduced by SNV normalization. While SNV was important for suppressing illumination variability, it also removed absolute reflectance magnitude, preserving only spectral shape. This likely contributed to some residual ambiguity between cracks and other structures with similar normalized signatures. A possible future improvement would be to combine normalized spectral shape with a small set of intensity-related features.

\subsection{Methodological Challenges and Data Curation Bottlenecks}
\noindent
Beyond the model comparisons, this study also exposed a major practical bottleneck in applied hyperspectral analysis: data curation. Transforming unstructured vineyard imagery into reliable pixel-level training data required substantial effort in annotation, cross-modal alignment, and iterative quality control.

\medskip\noindent
The first challenge was ground-truth creation. Reliable labels required both physical identification of cracked clusters in the vineyard and careful digital annotation within visually complex scenes. The second challenge was spatial alignment between RGB and HSI data. Because the two modalities differed in resolution and field of view, purely automatic transfer of coordinates was prone to geometric mismatch.

\medskip\noindent
For this reason, custom interactive tools became methodological necessities rather than simple convenience utilities. They enabled visual verification of ROI alignment, inspection of extracted spectra, and iterative refinement of the aggregation pipeline. In practice, this visual feedback loop was essential for tuning the trade-off between noise suppression and signal retention.

\medskip\noindent
Early attempts to reduce the annotation burden through unsupervised clustering were not successful. Under severe class imbalance, rare crack pixels were consistently absorbed into broader background structure, preventing stable isolation without prior labels. This outcome suggests that, for highly imbalanced real-world vineyard scenes, supervised annotation remains a necessary prerequisite for reliable crack detection.
